This is a serious adverse event, not a routine complaint, and it needs to move fast. Here's how I'd handle it.

## 1. Immediate triage (today)

Treat this as a potential Serious Adverse Event (SAE) right away. Under FDA's MoCRA rules, a "serious adverse event" includes any reaction requiring medical intervention — an ER visit and a prescription for steroids both qualify. That starts a regulatory clock, so don't sit on it.

Pull together everything you have on the case:
- Customer name and contact info
- Product name, SKU, and batch/lot number (get this from the customer if at all possible — check her order history in Shopify if she can't provide it)
- Date of use and date symptoms started
- Description of the reaction (facial swelling) and the medical response (ER visit, steroid prescription)
- Any photos she's willing to share
- Whether she still has the product/packaging

If the batch number isn't in hand yet, that's priority one — reach out to her directly (call is better than email for something this sensitive) and ask.

## 2. Log it

Open a complaint record for this now, even before you have every detail. Flag it internally as a potential SAE so it doesn't get processed like a normal complaint (wrong scent, shipping delay, etc.). Note the ER visit and steroid prescription explicitly — that's the language that matters for classification.

## 3. Classify

This needs a real SAE determination, not a guess. Generally, "serious" means: death, hospitalization, life-threatening event, significant disability/incapacity, congenital anomaly, or requires medical or surgical intervention to prevent one of those outcomes. A doctor prescribing steroids for facial swelling after topical product use reads as "required medical intervention" — which points toward reportable. Get whoever owns your regulatory/quality function to confirm the classification formally; don't self-diagnose this one.

## 4. Reporting clock

If this is confirmed as an SAE, MoCRA requires notifying FDA within 15 business days of the responsible person receiving the report (with an option to submit follow-up information within an additional 15 business days). The clock starts from when the company received notice — i.e., today, when the complaint came in — not from when the reaction happened. Get this on someone's calendar immediately with the due date spelled out.

## 5. Product/batch investigation

Notify whoever manages quality and manufacturing (KDC-One or whichever contract manufacturer made this batch) so they can pull the batch record, check for related complaints on the same lot, and see whether anything else shipped from that batch is still in the field. If other complaints come in on the same batch or ingredient, that changes the calculus toward a broader corrective action or recall discussion — but don't jump there yet on a single case.

## 6. Customer response

Respond to her directly and quickly. Express genuine concern, ask permission to gather the details above (batch number, photos, medical documentation if she's willing), and let her know you're looking into it. Don't admit fault or make promises about compensation until you know more, but don't go silent either — SAE customers who feel ignored escalate to their doctor, their lawyer, or social media.

## 7. Who needs to know

- Quality/regulatory lead — for SAE classification and the FDA filing
- Whoever owns the Castaway Cream formula/PD — in case this points to an ingredient or formulation issue
- Legal, if you have outside counsel on retainer, given the ER visit
- Leadership — a prescription-requiring adverse event on a flagship SKU is a founder-level heads-up, not something to handle quietly

## Bottom line

Don't try to resolve this like a normal complaint. Get the batch number, get a formal SAE classification from someone qualified to make that call, and get the 15-business-day FDA clock on the calendar the moment it's confirmed. Everything else — customer care, batch investigation, internal comms — runs in parallel, but the reporting clock is the one thing that can't slip.
