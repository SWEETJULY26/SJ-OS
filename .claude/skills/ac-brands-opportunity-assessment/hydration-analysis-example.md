# Worked Example: Ingestible Hydration Opportunity Assessment

The canonical worked example for this skill. The full ingestible
hydration assessment for SJS / AC Brands as it was originally produced,
with annotations explaining why each section is structured the way it
is.

When in doubt about how to structure an opportunity assessment, reference
this file. The pattern here is the pattern.

---

## Context

**Original prompt (paraphrased):** Ayesha brought up an instinct about
ingestible hydration as a possible bridge between her food authority and
SJS skincare. Half-formed, but specific — tropical Caribbean flavors,
collagen and skin-healthy ingredients, taste-forward. Greene had flagged
30K+ MOQs as a concern.

**The surface question:** Should SJS / AC Brands enter ingestible hydration?

**The underlying question:** Is there a brand-architecture path here that
solves the "why is the chef doing skincare" problem AC Brands has?

This reframe — surface vs. underlying — is the move that sets up the
entire assessment. The reader is told from the opening what's actually
being asked, which lets the analysis deliver against the right question.

---

## The original assessment

Below is the assessment as delivered, with annotations in `> Annotation:`
blocks explaining the structural choices.

---

### Opening reframe

> The bridge from food to skincare through ingestible hydration is exactly
> the kind of move that makes Ayesha's involvement in SJS feel obvious
> instead of confusing.

> **Annotation:** One sentence that names what's actually being asked.
> Not "should we enter the category" but "is this the move that solves
> the brand problem." This frames everything that follows.

---

### Part 1 — The real read on the market

**What the market actually looks like right now**

The category is hot and getting hotter. The hydration supplement market
is valued at USD 41.8 billion in 2026 and is forecasted to reach USD
89.4 billion by 2036, registering a CAGR of 7.9%. Beauty ingestibles
specifically are a smaller but faster-growing slice — the beauty
ingestible market will grow from $6.01 billion in 2025 to $6.84 billion
in 2026 at a compound annual growth rate (CAGR) of 13.9%, with
collagen-plus-electrolyte drink mixes being one of the most active
subcategories.

The thesis Ayesha is sketching — beauty-led hydration with a tropical,
food-driven point of view — is a real, validated category. She's not
inventing it. She'd be entering it.

> **Annotation:** Real numbers, cited. The category-validation paragraph
> answers "is this real" before the assessment goes anywhere else. The
> closing line ("she's not inventing it, she'd be entering it") sets up
> the competitive analysis that follows.

**Who's already there**

The signal launch she needs to know about: Sprinter, the vodka soda brand
founded by Kylie Jenner, is expanding into the functional beverage sector
with the launch of drink mix-ins designed to support skin health. The
new product, k2o by Sprinter, contains electrolytes with bioactive collagen
peptides and hyaluronic acid to support skin moisture and hydration.
Flavors include peach, watermelon lime and strawberry lychee. K2o launched
Wednesday on Sprinter's e-commerce website and TikTok Shop, with a 20-count
pack retailing for $39.99. That's $2/stick. Launched roughly a month ago.
Same celebrity-led-beauty-hydration thesis Ayesha is sketching, with
tropical-leaning flavors and a Verisol collagen claim.

The retail comp she needs to know about: In October 2025, Cure Hydration
launched its hydrating electrolyte drink mix at Ulta Beauty stores to
expand consumer access to ingestible wellness... [continued with Cure
detail]

The rest of the field is mostly mass/wellness-channel, not prestige beauty:
Applied Nutrition, Revive Collagen, Younique, Vital Proteins. Tropical
flavors are common. Caribbean-as-a-brand-story is essentially absent.

> **Annotation:** The competitive read is structured into camps, not
> listed flat. The two specific brands named (Sprinter and Cure) are
> the closest relevant launches — the brands to watch. The structural
> sort (celebrity-CPG vs. retail-comp vs. mass-rest-of-field) is what
> makes this analysis vs. listing.

**What no one in SJS's direct prestige-beauty peer set is doing**

[Names Rhode, Summer Fridays, Kosas, Glow Recipe — none are in
ingestibles. Names where the white space sits.]

> **Annotation:** Naming who's NOT in the space is as important as naming
> who is. The white space is the opportunity. The window is named
> implicitly — Sprinter launched a month ago, so the window is now.

---

### "The honest read on Ayesha's instinct" + "The honest read on the risks"

This pair of sections does something important: it delivers the honest
read on what's right about the instinct AND what's wrong, in two
explicitly-paired sections. The first section validates three specific
elements of Ayesha's thinking. The second names four specific risks.

> **Annotation:** This is a tone choice. When responding to a founder's
> instinct, the assessment must take it seriously and stress-test it.
> Validating only would be sycophancy. Stress-testing only would be
> dismissive. The structural pairing — "right about three things" + "the
> risks" — accomplishes both.

The four risks named: window closing, MOQs worse than estimated, channel
question (Sephora vs. Ulta), the "perfect bridge" framing only working
if the product actually performs.

---

### Part 2 — The honest read on the unit economics (in the second analysis)

The second pass added a deeper unit economics read with three subsections:

**MOQ reality.** Working assumption: 50,000 to 80,000 sticks per SKU per
run, so 200K to 320K total units committed up front for a 4-flavor launch.

**At $2.50–$3.00 wholesale, 200K units is roughly $500K–$600K in revenue
at full sell-through**, with $80K to $160K in inventory cash commitment
up front depending on formula complexity.

**COGS targets that actually clear the SJS framework.** A $14.99 6-pack
box at Ulta wholesales around $7.50. Landed COGS needs to be $1.80 to
$2.40 per box, or $0.30 to $0.40 per stick all-in. Tight but doable for
electrolyte-only. Adds up fast with collagen.

> **Annotation:** Real numbers, even when estimated. The working
> assumptions are shown explicitly. The "tight but doable for X, breaks
> with Y" framing is honest about where the math works and where it
> doesn't, and surfaces the formula-architecture decision (collagen-forward
> at premium pricing vs. electrolyte-plus-skin-actives at parity pricing)
> without forcing it.

---

### Part 3 — The hard parts (regulatory, ops, what you don't know)

**Regulatory is the part everyone underestimates, and it's actually the
thing that kills bad versions of this product.**

This isn't cosmetics. Dietary supplements fall under the Dietary Supplement
Health and Education Act (DSHEA), which treats supplements as a category
of food. This means supplements do not require FDA preapproval before
going to market — but they do require compliant labeling and proper good
manufacturing practices (GMPs).

Three things matter for SJS:

[Three numbered points: claims rules, mandatory disclaimer, third-party
testing question]

The aggregate regulatory + testing overhead probably adds $0.05 to $0.10
per stick to landed cost and 8 to 12 weeks to the launch timeline. That's
the honest number.

> **Annotation:** The regulatory section names specific rules (DSHEA,
> structure/function claims, the disclaimer) and gives a concrete number
> for cost and timeline impact. "That's the honest number" is the tone —
> direct, no hedging. This is the section that gets skipped or hand-waved
> in bad assessments. Naming the bodies is the whole point.

---

### Part 4 — The strategic question that hasn't been asked yet

> She framed this as "what if it's the thing that pulls it all together"
> for SJS. That framing has a hidden assumption — that the product needs
> to live under SJS to do that work. I'd push back on that.

Three real architectural options:

**Option A — SJS line extension.** "Sweet July Skin Hydration" or similar.
Pros: leverages SJS brand recognition, shared marketing, single P&L.
Cons: muddies the SJS brand, forces the framework to absorb a product
with totally different unit economics, complicates the wholesale
conversation. Rhode hasn't done this. Summer Fridays hasn't done this.
There's a reason.

**Option B — Sweet July sister brand under AC Brands.** Pros: clean brand
story, separate P&L, separate channel strategy, doesn't pollute SJS
framework. Cons: separate marketing budget, separate retail relationships,
probably can't ride SJS's Ulta launch, and AC Brands has to decide whether
it wants to be a multi-brand house — bigger question than this product.

**Option C — Ayesha's personal/lifestyle brand line.** Pros: maximum
flexibility, can lean fully into food credibility without skincare
baggage. Cons: doesn't help SJS at all, risks pulling Ayesha's marketing
oxygen away from skincare during the most fragile period.

My instinct, having read the analysis: Option B is the strongest, but
only if AC Brands has the capital and bandwidth for a second brand build,
which it currently doesn't. Option A is the path of least resistance and
probably the wrong one. Option C is the cleanest from a brand-architecture
standpoint and the worst from an SJS-investor perspective.

The conversation worth having — before any concept brief — is with
Danielle and Ayesha together: is AC Brands ready to be a multi-brand
house, and if not, is the right move to put a pin in this for 18 months
and revisit when SJS is post-Ulta-launch and stable?

> **Annotation:** This is the load-bearing section. The "hidden assumption"
> opener flags what the founder didn't see in their own framing. Three
> real options with honest tradeoffs. A clear recommendation (Option B)
> with the organizational caveat named (AC Brands has to commit to being
> multi-brand). The closing reframe escalates the conversation to the
> right altitude — not "should we make this product" but "what kind of
> company is AC Brands."

---

### Closing recommendation — what I'd actually recommend for the next 30 days

Three things, in order:

The first is a **brand architecture conversation, not a product brief.**
Ayesha doesn't need a concept brief yet — she needs Danielle and Soraya
to weigh in on which of A/B/C this is. Without that, any concept brief
I draft is solving the wrong problem.

The second is a **lightweight RFI to four co-mans** — StickPax (low-MOQ),
one mid-tier (NutraSeller, ActionPak, or similar), one prestige-grade
specialist, and one full-service formulator-of-record like Power Brands.
Three questions only: realistic MOQs at three pricing tiers, what a
Verisol vs. marine vs. plant-based vs. no-collagen formula actually costs
landed, and how long from kickoff to first production. That's a 4-week
exercise.

The third is **a competitive teardown of Cure** specifically — a $99 box
of all 5 SKUs from Ulta, taste-tested by Ayesha, Soraya, and Nicole,
scored on flavor, texture, dissolve, sweetness, packaging, and "would I
drink this every day."

> **Annotation:** Three actions, in priority order, each named and bounded.
> The first is a meeting (cheapest, gates everything else). The second
> is a 4-week scoping exercise (real but bounded). The third is a $99
> taste test (cheapest insight, highest leverage). Sequenced so the
> gating decision (brand architecture) happens first.

Closing with: "Want me to draft the co-man RFI brief, set up the Cure
tasting as an Asana task, or write the Danielle/Soraya briefing memo on
the brand architecture question? I'd start with the third — that's the
gating decision."

> **Annotation:** Offer specific deliverables. Recommend the highest-
> leverage one. Don't ask the user to figure out what to do with the
> assessment.

## Structural patterns to extract

A few structural patterns from this assessment that should carry forward:

**Pair "the right read" with "the honest risks."** When responding to a
founder's instinct, validate AND stress-test. Don't only do one.

**Name the closest relevant launch.** Sprinter and Cure aren't the whole
comp set — they're the brands to watch. Naming them specifically gives
the reader a concrete reference point.

**Surface the hidden assumption in the founder's framing.** "She framed
this as X. That framing has a hidden assumption — Y. I'd push back on
that." This is the move that elevates the assessment from tactical to
strategic.

**Recommend the strongest option, with the organizational caveat named.**
Don't dodge the recommendation. Don't recommend without naming what would
have to be true for it to work.

**Sequence the next 30 days by gating decision.** First action is the
meeting that gates everything else. Then the bounded research. Then the
quick-leverage insight. Don't list every possible next step.

**Close with specific deliverables on offer.** Not "let me know if I can
help." Three specific things, with a recommended starting point.
