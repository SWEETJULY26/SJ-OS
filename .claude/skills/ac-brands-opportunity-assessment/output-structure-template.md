# Conversational Output Structure

Detailed guidance for the 5-part conversational output template that
every opportunity assessment follows. This is the structural spine —
not a rigid form, but the shape every assessment hangs on.

The hydration analysis in `references/hydration-analysis-example.md`
is the canonical demonstration. Reference it alongside this template
when in doubt.

---

## The 5-part structure

```
Opening reframe          (1 paragraph)
Part 1 — Market read     (the structural shape of the opportunity)
Part 2 — Unit economics  (does the math work)
Part 3 — Hard parts      (operations, regulatory, what you don't know)
Part 4 — Strategic Q     (brand architecture, the unasked question)
Closing recommendation   (next 30 days, prioritized)
```

This walks the reader from "what is this thing" to "does the math work"
to "what's hard about it" to "how do we structure it" to "what do we do
next" in a single coherent arc.

---

## Opening reframe

**Goal:** One paragraph that names what's actually being asked.

**Why it matters:** The surface question is usually not the real question.
A founder asking "should we enter ingestible hydration" is actually
asking "is there a path here that solves the brand-architecture problem
my company has." The reframe surfaces that.

**What good looks like:**

- One paragraph, 3 to 5 sentences
- Names the surface question
- Names the underlying question if different
- Names any assumption baked in that's worth surfacing
- Sets up the analysis that follows

**What bad looks like:**

- Restating the question without reframing it
- A long preamble
- Hedging language ("there are many factors to consider")

## Part 1 — The real read on the market

**Goal:** Name the structural shape of the opportunity. Integrate Domain
1 (Consumer & Category) and Domain 2 (Competitive).

**Length:** 3 to 5 paragraphs typically. Longer if the comp set is
complex.

**Subsections that often appear:**

- "What the market actually looks like right now" — sizing, growth,
  durability of the trend
- "Who's already there" — the structural read of the comp set, sorted
  into camps, with the closest relevant launch named
- "Who's not there" — the white space, named explicitly

**What good looks like:**

- Real numbers wherever available, cited
- Comp set sorted into structural camps, not listed flat
- The closest relevant launch named — the brand to watch
- A clear read on the window — how long before the white space closes
- One non-obvious insight that the reader did not already know

**What bad looks like:**

- A list of competitors with no structural sorting
- Industry-report regurgitation without point of view
- Missing the celebrity-CPG comp set when the host brand is celebrity-led
- No real numbers

---

## Part 2 — The honest read on the unit economics

**Goal:** Name whether the math actually works. Integrate Domain 3 (PD),
Domain 4 (Operations), Domain 5 (Margin).

**Length:** 2 to 4 paragraphs typically.

**Subsections that often appear:**

- "MOQ reality" — what the realistic minimums look like
- "COGS targets that actually clear the framework" — the price tier
  required to make the math work
- "The cash commitment at launch" — the inventory dollars tied up

**What good looks like:**

- Real numbers, even when estimated (always show the working assumption)
- Honest when the math doesn't work at the assumed price points
- Names the cost traps specific to this category (claims, testing,
  secondary packaging)
- Names the framework implications when the host brand has one

**What bad looks like:**

- Headline COGS without all-in landed cost
- Optimistic-case math without stress-testing
- Avoiding the framework conversation
- Vague unit-economics handwaving ("the margins should work")

---

## Part 3 — The hard parts (regulatory, ops, what you don't know)

**Goal:** Name the bodies. The things that get underestimated. Integrate
Domain 4 (Ops, where it gets harder) and Domain 7 (Regulatory).

**Length:** 2 to 4 paragraphs typically.

**Subsections that often appear:**

- "Regulatory is the part everyone underestimates" — the FDA regime,
  claims rules, mandatory disclosures, testing requirements
- "Operations is different than you think" — 3PL capability, planogram
  fit, retailer audit requirements
- "What we don't currently know how to do" — explicit statement of
  the capability gaps

**What good looks like:**

- Specific, named regulatory rules (DSHEA disclaimer, structure/function
  claims, etc.)
- Specific operational implications (Sephora doesn't do ingestibles,
  3PL needs FEFO, etc.)
- Honest about the cost and timeline of regulatory and ops setup
- Names whether existing partners (regulatory, 3PL, suppliers) can
  handle this

**What bad looks like:**

- "Regulatory is complex" without specifics
- Assuming current infrastructure extends without checking
- Skipping regulatory entirely
- Treating ops and regulatory as someone else's problem

---

## Part 4 — The strategic question that hasn't been asked yet

**Goal:** Surface the brand-architecture question. Name the 2 to 4 real
paths and their tradeoffs. Domain 6 (Brand) leading.

**Length:** 4 to 6 paragraphs typically. This is often the longest part
of the assessment because it's the most decision-relevant.

**Structure that works:**

- Open by naming the founder's framing and the hidden assumption inside
  it (e.g., "She framed this as 'what if it's the thing that pulls it
  all together' for SJS. That framing has a hidden assumption — that
  the product needs to live under SJS to do that work.")
- Lay out 2 to 4 real architectural options
- For each option: name it, name the pros, name the cons honestly,
  name the implication
- Close with a recommendation — which option is strongest, with
  reasoning

**What good looks like:**

- The hidden assumption named explicitly
- 2 to 4 real options, not a binary
- Honest tradeoffs for each — including for the recommended option
- A clear recommendation with reasoning
- Acknowledgment of organizational implications (e.g., "this requires
  AC Brands to commit to being a multi-brand house, which is a bigger
  question than this product")

**What bad looks like:**

- Skipping this section entirely
- Defaulting to line extension as the obvious answer
- Listing options without recommending
- Treating the brand-architecture question as a marketing question
  instead of a corporate one

## Closing recommendation

**Goal:** Specific, actionable, prioritized next steps for the next
30 days.

**Length:** 3 to 5 short paragraphs or a short numbered list with
prose context.

**Structure that works:**

- Name 2 to 4 specific actions, in priority order
- For each action: what it is, why it's the right next step, who owns
  it, what it costs (in time or dollars)
- Close with an offer to draft, scope, or set up specific deliverables

**What good looks like:**

- Specific actions ("scope an RFI to four co-mans", "set up a Cure
  tasting", "draft the Danielle/Soraya brief on brand architecture")
- Sequenced — what comes first, what comes second, what depends on
  what
- Owned — names a person where possible
- Bounded — time-boxed to the next 30 days, not "eventually"

**What bad looks like:**

- "Consider further analysis" without naming what
- A list of every possible next step without prioritization
- No owners
- Vague timelines

## Tone, voice, and writing rules

The opportunity assessment is written in the AC Brands voice. Specific
constraints (also in the SKILL.md anti-AI-jargon block):

- No banned words: delve, leverage, utilize, facilitate, optimize,
  implement, streamline, empower, harness, unlock, unleash, foster,
  elevate, showcase, robust, seamless, comprehensive, vital, crucial,
  pivotal, multifaceted, nuanced, intricate, ever-evolving, cutting-edge,
  innovative, dynamic, holistic, profound, meticulous, daunting, tapestry,
  realm, landscape (metaphor), ecosystem (metaphor), paradigm, synergy,
  methodology (in prose, fine in headers), journey, voyage
- No banned openers ("In today's fast-paced world," "When it comes to,"
  "At the end of the day")
- No banned transitions (furthermore, moreover, additionally, indeed,
  notably, importantly, interestingly, subsequently, consequently)
- No banned closings ("In conclusion," "In summary," "Ultimately,"
  "At its core")
- No "It's not X — it's Y" reframes — state the point directly
- Default to paragraphs, not bullets — only bullet for genuine lists
  (parallel, 3+ items)
- No bolded lead words on bullets ("Clarity:", "Impact:")
- Em dashes preferred over hyphens
- Vary sentence lengths
- Be concrete

The voice is direct, opinionated, and human. It reads like a smart
person talking to a colleague, not like a consulting deck.

## Length calibration

Quick assessments (gut-check level): 800 to 1,500 words.

Standard assessments (most): 2,000 to 3,500 words.

High-stakes assessments (founder-facing, multi-million-dollar implications):
3,500 to 5,000+ words. The hydration analysis is in this band.

Length scales to question complexity, not to expectation. A simple
question gets a short answer. A complex question gets a long one. Do
not pad to hit a length target.

## Citations and sourcing

Every factual claim from web research should be cited inline using the
`` format. The skill defaults to web search and
web fetch as primary research tools, and the citations let the user
verify and dig deeper.

Paraphrase, do not quote. Direct quotes should be used sparingly and
kept short (under 15 words per quote, never more than one quote per
source). When in doubt, paraphrase.

Real numbers wherever possible. When estimating, show the working
assumption clearly.
