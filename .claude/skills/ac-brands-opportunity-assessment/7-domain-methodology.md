# 7-Domain Synthesis Methodology

Deep reference for each of the seven domains an opportunity assessment must
integrate. For each domain: what to research, where to look, what good
output looks like, common traps.

Read this file at the start of any opportunity assessment. The seven domains
are walked in order — each one informs the next.

---

## Domain 1 — Consumer & Category Reality

**The question:** Is the demand real? Is it durable? Is the trend a fad
or a structural shift? Who is the consumer, what are they actually buying,
and is the category growing, flat, or declining?

**What to research:**

- Category size and growth rate (CAGR), ideally with a 5-year and 10-year view
- Consumer demographic and psychographic detail — who buys this, why, how often
- Trend trajectory — is the category accelerating, plateauing, or peaking?
- Repeat purchase behavior — fad categories spike on first-purchase, durable
  categories show repeat
- Adjacent category signals — what's happening in food, wellness, beauty,
  lifestyle that supports or undermines the thesis

**Where to look:**

- Industry research firms: Circana (formerly NPD), Mintel, Statista, Euromonitor
- Trade press: BeautyMatter, Business of Beauty (BoF), WWD, Glossy, Modern Retail,
  Beauty Independent
- Consulting firm reports: McKinsey, BCG, Bain on consumer/beauty/wellness
- Earnings calls and investor decks from public comp-set companies
- Google Trends for trajectory direction
- Subreddit and TikTok ethnography for early signal

**What good output looks like:**

A 1 to 2 paragraph synthesis that names: the size of the category, the
growth rate, the durability of the trend, who the consumer is, and one
non-obvious insight about consumer behavior in this space. Cited where
possible. Real numbers wherever available.

**Common traps:**

- Confusing earned media value (EMV) with actual sales — viral does not equal
  selling
- Reading a trend report as gospel — most are commissioned by stakeholders
  with a thesis to sell
- Mistaking a fad-shaped trajectory for durable demand — collagen drinks
  spiked in 2018, crashed by 2020, and only stabilized as a real category
  in 2023+. Trajectory shape matters
- Skipping repeat-purchase analysis — first-purchase data alone is misleading

## Domain 2 — Competitive Reality

**The question:** Who is already in the space? What is their positioning,
pricing, channel strategy, and growth rate? Where are the gaps? What is
the white space and how long is the window?

**What to research:**

- The full comp set, sorted into camps (e.g., performance vs. luxury,
  prestige vs. mass, DTC-only vs. omnichannel)
- Pricing architecture across the comp set — opening price, hero price,
  bundle price
- Channel strategy — DTC, Sephora, Ulta, Amazon, grocery, specialty
- Growth rates and recent funding/acquisitions
- Recent launches in the space (last 12 months) — who's entering, with what
- Review velocity at retail (Ulta, Sephora, Amazon) — strongest leading
  indicator of sell-through
- Promotional activity — how much and how often discounted

**Where to look:**

- Retailer site audits — Ulta, Sephora, Amazon Premium Beauty, Target
- Brand DTC sites for full pricing architecture
- Crunchbase, PitchBook (when available) for funding and growth signals
- Trade press for launch announcements and category commentary
- Tribe Dynamics, Traackr, or similar for EMV when available
- Yipit Data, Earnest Analytics, Bloomberg Second Measure for spend data
  when available
- TikTok and Instagram for organic reach signals

**What good output looks like:**

A structural read of the competitive set — typically 2 to 4 camps with
specific brands placed in each. Names the white space. Names the closest
relevant launch (the brand to watch). Cites review velocity or sales
proxies where available. Names the window — how long before the white
space closes.

**Common traps:**

- Listing competitors without sorting them into structural camps — a list
  is not analysis
- Missing the celebrity-CPG comp set when the host brand is celebrity-led
- Treating prestige and mass as one market — they aren't
- Ignoring international comp signal (Asia-Pacific is often 18 to 24
  months ahead of US on beauty trends)
- Failing to name the window — every category has a closing window for
  white-space entry

## Domain 3 — PD Reality

**The question:** Can AC Brands actually make this? Who would manufacture
it? What does the supplier landscape look like? What's the formulation
complexity? What is a realistic timeline from concept to shelf?

**What to research:**

- The supplier landscape for the relevant manufacturing type (skincare
  co-mans look very different from supplement co-mans, very different
  from beverage co-packers)
- Realistic MOQs at three pricing tiers (entry, mid, prestige)
- Formulation complexity — what actives or claims drive cost
- Timeline benchmarks from concept brief to first shipment
- Whether AC Brands' existing supplier network has capability or whether
  net-new sourcing is required

**Where to look:**

- Trade publications for the relevant category (Nutraceuticals World for
  supplements, BevNet for beverages, Cosmetics & Toiletries for skincare)
- Light-touch reference to `sjs-pd-system` for current-state SJS supplier
  knowledge
- Co-man directories: ContractFood, ThomasNet, supplement co-man directories
- Industry conferences: SupplySide West, IBA, Natural Products Expo
- Direct supplier outreach when the assessment warrants it (RFI scoping)

**What good output looks like:**

A 1 to 2 paragraph read on whether the manufacturing is hard, who would
do it, what the realistic MOQs are, and what a real timeline looks like.
Names whether existing suppliers have capability or whether net-new
sourcing is required. Honest about complexity.

**Common traps:**

- Assuming existing supplier network can extend into adjacent categories
  (it usually cannot — KDC-One does skincare, not supplements)
- Quoting low-MOQ boutique numbers as realistic for a prestige launch
  (they aren't — prestige retail vendor audits eliminate boutique co-mans)
- Underestimating timeline by 30 to 50 percent — every PD timeline slips
- Ignoring the regulatory side of PD (some categories require third-party
  testing, GMP-certified facilities, or specific supplier credentials
  that narrow the field significantly)

## Domain 4 — Operations Reality

**The question:** What does this do to the supply chain, 3PL, retail
relationships, and inventory commitment? Are MOQs realistic? Does this
fit existing ops infrastructure or require new infrastructure?

**What to research:**

- 3PL capability — does OC3PL handle this product type (storage temp,
  shelf life tracking, FEFO, hazmat, etc.)?
- Retailer planogram fit — does this product fit on existing shelves at
  the host brand's retail accounts, or does it require a new shop section?
- Inventory cash commitment — total cash tied up at launch given realistic
  MOQs
- Forecasting risk — first-launch forecasting in a new category is harder
- Returns and reverse logistics — beverage and supplement returns are
  different from skincare
- Cross-border / customs implications if manufacturing is offshore

**Where to look:**

- Light-touch reference to `ac-brands-ops-system` for current-state
  Ops context
- Retailer planogram observation (Ulta, Sephora, Target — what's on which
  shelf, what's not)
- 3PL benchmarking — Logiwa for FEFO and shelf-life tracking, Flowspace
  for cold chain
- Industry benchmarks for inventory turn by category

**What good output looks like:**

A 1 to 2 paragraph read on whether the operational infrastructure is
ready, what new infrastructure is required if not, and what the cash
commitment looks like at launch. Names the planogram question explicitly.

**Common traps:**

- Assuming the 3PL can handle a new product type without confirming
- Forgetting that retailers have separate planogram sections for skincare
  vs. ingestibles vs. wellness — Sephora generally does not carry
  ingestibles, Ulta does
- Underestimating the cash commitment of MOQ inventory
- Missing the forecasting risk on first-launch in a new category

## Domain 5 — Margin Reality

**The question:** What are the unit economics? Does it clear the relevant
brand's margin framework? What's the channel-net at each prospective
channel? Where are the cost traps?

**What to research:**

- Realistic landed COGS targets for a competitive product in this space
- Pricing architecture from the comp set (entry, hero, bundle prices)
- Channel-net margin at each prospective channel (DTC, wholesale tiers,
  Amazon)
- Whether the math clears the host brand's margin framework
- Where the cost traps are — in beauty, claims and clinicals can double
  COGS; in supplements, third-party testing adds 5 to 10 percent

**Where to look:**

- Light-touch reference to `sjs-margin-architect` family for SJS framework
  rules when SJS is the host
- Comp-set pricing data from retailer audits
- Trade press cost commentary
- Industry benchmarks for COGS-to-MSRP ratios by category

**What good output looks like:**

A clean 1 to 2 paragraph read on whether the unit economics work at
realistic price points and realistic COGS. Names the price tier required
to clear the framework. Names the cost traps. Honest when the math doesn't
work — does not bend the framework to make it work.

**Common traps:**

- Quoting headline COGS without including all-in landed cost (freight,
  duty, testing, packaging, labels, secondary packaging)
- Assuming margin framework can absorb a different product type — it
  cannot, frameworks are calibrated to specific cost structures
- Ignoring channel-mix effects on blended margin
- Treating MSRP as fixed when it's actually the lever you have to use to
  make the framework work

## Domain 6 — Brand Reality

**The question:** Does this strengthen or muddy the host brand's story?
What is the brand-architecture question (line extension vs. sister brand
vs. new brand)? Does the founder's authority extend here naturally or
does the move feel forced?

**What to research:**

- Less research, more interpretation — this domain is judgment-heavy
- Comp-set brand-architecture choices — when have prestige beauty brands
  successfully extended into adjacent categories, and when have they
  failed?
- Founder credibility and category fit — does the founder have authority
  in the new space, or does it feel like a stretch?
- Existing brand equity and what it can absorb without dilution

**Where to look:**

- Trade press analysis of brand extensions (the case studies on Glossier
  Body, Goop, Honest Company are instructive)
- Founder interviews and brand equity research
- Internal brand strategy docs when available

**What good output looks like:**

A 1 to 2 paragraph read on the brand-architecture question. Names 2 to 4
real options (line extension, sister brand, separate brand, license/
collaboration) with tradeoffs for each. Names which option strengthens
the host brand and which dilutes it. Honest when the founder's authority
does not extend.

**Common traps:**

- Defaulting to line extension because it's the path of least resistance
  (it's usually the wrong answer)
- Treating brand fit as binary (it is a spectrum and depends on the
  specific product and positioning)
- Ignoring the dilution risk — every line extension takes marketing
  oxygen from the core
- Assuming celebrity authority is unlimited — it is highly category-
  specific

## Domain 7 — Regulatory Reality

**The question:** What is the regulatory regime? FDA cosmetic vs. dietary
supplement vs. food vs. drug? What can you say on the label? In marketing?
Are there testing or certification requirements?

**What to research:**

- Which regulatory regime governs the product (FDA cosmetic, FDA dietary
  supplement under DSHEA, FDA food, FDA OTC drug, FTC for advertising)
- Specific claims rules — what constitutes a structure/function claim
  vs. a drug claim
- Mandatory label disclosures
- Third-party testing requirements (formal or de facto)
- State-level rules (California Prop 65, NY supplement rules)
- Whether existing regulatory partners (e.g., Pedrero) have capability in
  this regime

**Where to look:**

- FDA guidance documents (fda.gov)
- FTC guides for advertising claims
- Industry trade associations (CRN for supplements, PCPC for cosmetics)
- Trade press regulatory commentary
- Direct conversation with regulatory counsel for any high-stakes
  assessment

**What good output looks like:**

A 1 to 2 paragraph read on the regulatory regime, the claims rules, the
mandatory disclosures, and the testing requirements. Names whether
existing regulatory partners can handle this or whether new partners
are needed. Honest about regulatory cost and timeline.

**Common traps:**

- Assuming "no FDA preapproval" means easy — it doesn't
- Underestimating claims rules — most beauty brands write copy that would
  not survive FDA scrutiny in a dietary supplement context
- Forgetting state-level rules
- Skipping third-party testing cost (formal NSF/USP certification, ongoing
  heavy-metal testing, etc.)
- Assuming current regulatory partner has capability across all regimes
  (cosmetic regulatory and supplement regulatory are different practices)

## Synthesis — integrating the seven

The output of an opportunity assessment is not seven separate sections.
It is one synthesized argument that names tensions across domains and
resolves them.

The synthesis pattern that works:

1. **Open with the structural read** — what camps does the comp set
   sort into, where is the white space (Domains 1 + 2 integrated)
2. **Stress-test with the unit economics** — does the math actually
   work (Domains 3 + 4 + 5 integrated)
3. **Name the operational and regulatory bodies** — what's hard about
   doing this (Domains 4 + 7 surfaced)
4. **Surface the strategic question** — brand architecture, the question
   the founder hasn't asked yet (Domain 6 leading)
5. **Close with the recommendation** — what to do in the next 30 days

The hydration analysis in `references/hydration-analysis-example.md`
demonstrates this pattern end to end. When in doubt about how to
integrate, reference that example.

## Common cross-domain traps

A few traps that show up across domains and are worth naming:

**The "everyone says it's growing" trap.** Industry reports always say
the category is growing. Always check the report's commissioner — most
are paid for by the same brands trying to raise money in the space.

**The "COGS doesn't include everything" trap.** Headline COGS is rarely
landed COGS. Always pressure-test for freight, duty, testing, secondary
packaging, and unit-level marketing costs (samples, gifting).

**The "brand fit feels obvious" trap.** Brand fit is the easiest thing
to talk yourself into. Pressure-test against actual examples — when has
this kind of extension worked, when has it failed?

**The "we'll figure regulatory out later" trap.** Regulatory is the
single most underestimated domain in opportunity assessments. Build it
into Phase 2 always, never as an afterthought.

**The "founder authority extends naturally" trap.** Founder authority
is highly category-specific. Goop works in wellness, not in tech. Beats
worked in headphones, not in everything. Test the specific extension,
not a general principle.
